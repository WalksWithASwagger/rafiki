# HOPECODE Prompts: Concept Mapping

Example prompts for mapping ideas, relationships, and complex concepts using HOPECODE aesthetic. These prompts focus on understanding connections and exploring idea spaces.

## 1. Project Ecosystem - Stakeholder Web

**For:** Project planning, stakeholder analysis
**Aspect:** 1:1
**File name:** project-stakeholder-web.png

**Prompt:**
> A relationship network showing all stakeholders in a community tech project. Center shows the project itself as a core node. Radiating outward: community members who'll use it, developers building it, funders supporting it, partner organizations, local government, existing tech alternatives, commercial competitors, academic researchers, media who might cover it. Connections show relationships - some collaborative, some tense, some potential. Line thickness indicates relationship strength. Include zones of influence: who has formal power, who has practical veto, who holds crucial knowledge, who controls resources, who speaks for community. Marginal notes capture relationship nuances: shared history, power dynamics, trust levels, communication channels. Show how some stakeholders bridge different groups. Include temporal dimension: relationships that exist now vs. those that need building. The overall structure should reveal both the complexity and the strategic opportunities - who needs to be involved, what coalitions make sense, where tensions might arise. Not a clean org chart but an honest map of the actual social terrain.

## 2. Skill Evolution - Capability Branches

**For:** Professional development planning, skill assessment
**Aspect:** 16:9
**File name:** skill-evolution-map.png

**Prompt:**
> A branching tree diagram showing how skills build on each other over time. Roots represent foundational capabilities - curiosity, pattern recognition, communication, persistence. Main trunk shows core competencies that enable specialization. Branches extend outward showing specialized skills, with smaller twigs for specific tools or techniques. Some branches grow toward each other, showing how distant skills eventually connect. Include seasonal markers showing how different skills were developed at different life stages. Color zones represent skill domains: technical, creative, interpersonal, strategic. Thickness of branches indicates depth of mastery. Some branches are mature and strong, others are new growth, some are dormant but could be reactivated. Marginal annotations tell micro-stories: how a skill was acquired, who taught it, breakthrough moments, connections discovered. Include both credentialed skills (degrees, certifications) and informal knowledge (self-taught, community-learned, experience-based). The overall structure should show that expertise isn't linear - it's organic, branching, interconnected. Different paths are possible from the same roots.

## 3. Idea Genealogy - Where Thoughts Come From

**For:** Research mapping, intellectual history
**Aspect:** 1:1
**File name:** idea-genealogy-map.png

**Prompt:**
> A genealogical tree showing the ancestry of a current idea or framework. Present moment at top showing current thinking. Branches extending downward and backward show intellectual influences: books read, thinkers engaged, conversations that shifted perspective, experiences that challenged assumptions, communities that shaped worldview, teachers and mentors, opposing arguments that refined thinking. Some branches are direct influences (explicitly cited), others are ambient (absorbed from culture), others are reactive (formed in opposition to). Include zones representing different knowledge traditions: academic scholarship, activist organizing, spiritual practice, artistic expression, lived experience. Show cross-pollination where different traditions informed each other. Marginal notes capture specific aha moments, quotes that stuck, debates that clarified, practices that embodied the ideas. Include acknowledgment of unattributed influences and the limits of tracing origin - ideas emerge from collective meaning-making, not individual genius. The overall structure reveals that even "original" thinking is always in conversation with what came before. Ideas have context, history, and community roots.

## 4. Values Constellation - What Actually Matters

**For:** Personal reflection, organizational culture work
**Aspect:** 1:1
**File name:** values-constellation-map.png

**Prompt:**
> A constellation map of values showing what actually guides decisions, not just what we claim to value. Larger nodes for values that consistently inform action, smaller nodes for aspirational values not yet integrated. Connections show how values support or tension with each other: autonomy and community, excellence and sustainability, innovation and tradition, justice and pragmatism. Some values cluster together naturally, others exist in productive tension. Color zones indicate value origins: family upbringing, cultural context, personal experience, intentional cultivation, community norms. Include temporal dimension: values that have been constant vs. those that evolved, childhood values outgrown, new values being integrated. Marginal annotations give examples of each value in action - specific decisions, difficult tradeoffs, moments of clarity or compromise. Show how context shifts which values take precedence - different settings activate different value hierarchies. The overall structure should be honest rather than aspirational - revealing actual operative values, not just stated ones. Include space for values in tension without trying to resolve them - moral complexity is real.

## 5. Community Map - The Invisible Infrastructure

**For:** Community organizing, social capital mapping
**Aspect:** 16:9
**File name:** community-infrastructure-map.png

**Prompt:**
> A community map showing the invisible infrastructure that makes collective life possible. Physical space shown as background - buildings, streets, parks - but foreground shows the social infrastructure: where people gather informally, who organizes events, which spaces feel safe to different groups, information flows and communication channels, who bridges different subcommunities, resources shared informally, skills and knowledge distributed throughout the network, care work and mutual aid, conflict resolution pathways, decision-making spaces, cultural traditions and shared rituals. Connections show relationships and resource flows. Nodes vary in size based on community significance (not institutional power). Include zones of belonging - where different groups feel at home, where boundaries exist, where overlap happens. Marginal notes identify key community holders: the person who always shows up, the elder with memory, the connector who knows everyone, the organizer who mobilizes. Show both strengths (dense connections, diverse skills, established traditions) and gaps (isolated members, unmet needs, excluded groups). The overall structure reveals what actually makes community work - not institutions but relationships, practices, and distributed care.

## 6. Decision Tree - The Path Not Taken

**For:** Reflecting on major decisions, exploring alternatives
**Aspect:** 9:16
**File name:** decision-tree-paths.png

**Prompt:**
> A decision tree showing a major life choice and the paths branching from it. A key decision point in the center with multiple paths extending outward. One path is bold and colored - the path taken, showing how it unfolded with subsequent choices and experiences. Other paths shown fainter, ghost-like - the roads not traveled, what might have been. Include annotations showing what influenced the decision: fears, hopes, advice received, circumstances, values, random chance. Along the taken path, mark moments of confirmation (this was right) and doubt (should I have chosen differently). For untaken paths, show what was sacrificed and what was avoided - every choice includes both loss and gain. Include zones representing different life dimensions affected: career, relationships, location, identity, politics, creative expression. Some untaken paths eventually converge back with the taken one - different routes to similar destinations. Others diverge radically. Marginal notes acknowledge uncertainty - we can never know what the other paths held. The overall feeling should be contemplative rather than regretful - honoring the complexity of consequential choices. Not about right or wrong, but about how we navigate uncertainty.

## 7. Knowledge Garden - Areas of Inquiry

**For:** Research planning, intellectual exploration
**Aspect:** 1:1
**File name:** knowledge-garden-map.png

**Prompt:**
> A garden map of knowledge domains and areas of active inquiry. Different regions represent fields of investigation: well-cultivated garden beds (areas of depth), wild growth sections (emergent interests), fallow fields (dormant but could be revived), seed beds (just beginning to explore), compost heaps (failed experiments that still feed future growth). Pathways between regions show how different areas of inquiry connect and inform each other. Some plants are perennials (enduring interests), others are annuals (temporary deep dives), others are volunteers (unexpected growth). Include seasonal cycles - what's in bloom now, what's being harvested, what's being planted for future seasons. Zones represent different types of knowing: theoretical understanding, practical application, creative expression, teaching/sharing, collaborative inquiry. Marginal annotations note what questions drive each area, what mysteries remain, what breakthroughs happened, what collaborators cultivate this space. Show both individual plots and communal spaces where collective inquiry happens. The overall structure reveals knowledge as living system requiring tending - not just accumulation but active cultivation, pruning, cross-pollination, and regeneration.
