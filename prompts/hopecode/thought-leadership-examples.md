# HOPECODE Prompts: Thought Leadership

Example prompts for radical thought leadership content using HOPECODE aesthetic. These prompts focus on alternative frameworks, systems thinking, and visionary perspectives.

## 1. Anti-Corporate Framework - The True Cost Diagram

**For:** Article header, critical analysis piece
**Aspect:** 16:9
**File name:** true-cost-framework.png

**Prompt:**
> A framework diagram showing the hidden costs of "scale-first" business models. Center shows a gleaming corporate tower, but underground roots reveal extraction: depleted communities, burned-out workers, environmental damage, cultural homogenization. Surface level shows metrics that get measured (revenue, users, growth), underground shows what gets ignored (care work, ecological debt, social fabric erosion). Connections between surface and underground reveal how one feeds on the other. Include zones of color - corporate metrics in cold blues, human/ecological costs in warm earth tones and ash. Marginal annotations call out specific examples, quote corporate jargon contrasted with lived reality. The overall structure should feel revelatory - making visible what capitalism works to keep hidden. Not angry, but clear-eyed and refusing to look away.

## 2. Systems Thinking - The Mycelial Internet

**For:** Essay illustration, alternative tech vision
**Aspect:** 1:1
**File name:** mycelial-internet-vision.png

**Prompt:**
> A systems map reimagining the internet as mycelial network rather than corporate platform. Show information and connection flowing through decentralized nodes, each node representing a community or collective rather than a company. Thick connections between nodes that share values and collaborate, thinner threads to more distant communities. Some nodes act as hubs but don't control the network. Include zones showing different network qualities: gift economy regions, common-pool resources, mutual aid connections. Contrast this organic structure with ghost outlines of corporate platforms (suggested but fading away) - centralized, extractive, surveilling. Marginal notes describe how this actually works: protocols not platforms, federation not monopoly, community governance not shareholders. The overall feel should be hopeful and pragmatic - not utopian but actually buildable. Include small working examples: Mastodon, community mesh networks, solidarity economy experiments.

## 3. Future Vision - Solarpunk City Metabolism

**For:** Speculative design piece, climate futures article
**Aspect:** 16:9
**File name:** solarpunk-city-metabolism.png

**Prompt:**
> A metabolic diagram of a solarpunk city showing flows of energy, materials, food, waste, water, and care as interconnected cycles. Solar and wind energy flowing into battery storage and direct use. Food growing in vertical gardens, rooftop farms, neighborhood plots - with composting loops returning nutrients. Water cycling through rain harvesting, greywater systems, bioswales. Waste streams becoming inputs for other processes - nothing is garbage, just resources in transition. Care work visible and valued - childcare collectives, elder care networks, mental health support - shown as foundational infrastructure like roads or power. Include both high-tech (solar panels, sensors) and low-tech (hand tools, seed saving, traditional knowledge). The city should feel alive and breathing, with organic and technological elements integrated seamlessly. Marginal notes describe policy shifts that enable this: shortened work weeks, universal basic services, community land trusts. Not a blueprint but an invitation - this could be built, here's how it works.

## 4. Critical Analysis - The Algorithm Theater

**For:** Tech criticism article, algorithmic accountability piece
**Aspect:** 1:1
**File name:** algorithm-theater-diagram.png

**Prompt:**
> A theatrical stage diagram exposing how algorithms perform objectivity while encoding power. The stage shows an algorithm as a black box in center with inputs flowing in from left (data, training sets, optimization targets) and outputs flowing right (decisions, recommendations, automated judgments). But pull back the curtain to reveal: biased training data, optimization for engagement over truth, hidden human labor doing content moderation, venture capital investors pulling strings, regulatory capture, surveillance infrastructure. Above the stage, the audience (users) shown as both spectators and performers - their data feeds the system while the system shapes their choices. Marginal annotations expose specific examples: facial recognition trained on biased datasets, recommendation engines radicalizing viewers, gig economy apps controlling workers. Include symbols of theater - masks, spotlights, strings of a puppet show - to emphasize the performance of neutrality. The overall message: algorithms aren't objective mathematics, they're power structures wearing math's costume. Make the invisible visible.

## 5. Transformation Framework - From Empire to Ecosystem

**For:** Decolonial framework article, organizational transformation
**Aspect:** 16:9
**File name:** empire-to-ecosystem-transformation.png

**Prompt:**
> A transformation diagram showing the shift from empire logic to ecosystem logic across multiple dimensions. Left side shows empire structures: hierarchical pyramids, extraction flows, monoculture thinking, scarcity mindset, borders and exclusion, linear progress narratives. Right side shows ecosystem alternatives: distributed networks, regenerative cycles, biodiversity of approaches, abundance through sharing, porous membranes instead of walls, spiral growth that includes the past. Between them, not a straight arrow but a winding, difficult path showing the actual work of transformation: unlearning, grief, accountability, repair, building new muscle memory. Include specific dimensions like governance (from command-and-control to consent-based), economics (from extraction to reciprocity), knowledge (from expert monopoly to collective intelligence), time (from speed/scale to seasonal rhythms). Marginal notes acknowledge this isn't easy or automatic - transformation requires intention, practice, and collective commitment. The ecosystem side should feel more alive, textured, complex - not simpler but more sophisticated. Include examples from Indigenous governance, solidarity economy, cooperative movements.

## 6. Power Mapping - Where the Levers Actually Are

**For:** Organizing strategy article, movement building piece
**Aspect:** 1:1
**File name:** power-levers-map.png

**Prompt:**
> A power mapping diagram showing where change actually happens, countering the myth of individual consumer choice. Center shows the system we want to change (pick one: tech monopolies, fossil fuel industry, housing crisis, healthcare system). Radiating outward, show different points of leverage with honest assessment of impact. Individual choices (voting, consumption) shown accurately as small levers with limited direct impact but potential for consciousness raising. More powerful levers shown larger: collective action, union organizing, mutual aid networks, community control, policy battles, shareholder activism, infrastructure alternatives, cultural shift. Show connections between levers - how consciousness raising feeds organizing, how mutual aid builds power for policy wins, how alternatives demonstrate what's possible. Include zones showing where different groups have leverage: workers can strike, communities can occupy, investors can divest, technologists can build alternatives, culture workers can shift narratives. Marginal notes give concrete examples: successful campaigns, hard-won victories, ongoing struggles. The overall message: change isn't impossible, but it requires collective power, strategy, and persistence. Individual guilt is a trap - we need organized action.

## 7. Knowledge Web - Decolonizing Ways of Knowing

**For:** Epistemology article, Indigenous knowledge systems piece
**Aspect:** 1:1
**File name:** decolonizing-knowledge-web.png

**Prompt:**
> A knowledge web showing multiple ways of knowing beyond Western academic epistemology. Center region shows dominant paradigm: written text, peer review, individual authorship, abstraction, universal claims, separation of knower from known. Branching outward, show alternative knowledge systems: Indigenous oral traditions, embodied practice knowledge, collective memory, land-based learning, spiritual/intuitive knowing, artistic expression, elder wisdom, direct experience. Don't arrange hierarchically - show as interconnected web where different ways of knowing complement and sometimes conflict with each other. Include bridges between systems where they've successfully connected, and gaps where Western systems have failed to honor other ways. Marginal notes give examples: how Indigenous science understood ecology for millennia before Western biology caught up, how craft knowledge exceeds what can be written down, how spiritual practices access truths inaccessible to materialist frameworks. Include symbols of different knowledge transmission: books alongside oral storytelling, equations alongside ceremony, diagrams alongside demonstration. The overall message: knowledge is plural, not singular. Western academic knowledge has value but doesn't have monopoly on truth. Decolonizing knowledge means making room for multiple epistemologies without forcing them into Western frameworks.
